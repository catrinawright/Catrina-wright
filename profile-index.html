Professional Background

Not a Career Change.
A Career Convergence.

My path to special education is not a career change. It is a career convergence. Every role I held before the classroom — Research Assistant at the University of Chicago, Probation Officer at Cook County, Criminal Justice and Psychology scholar at Chicago State University — was building the same core competency: reading complex systems, advocating for vulnerable people inside those systems, and making evidence-based decisions under pressure. That is precisely what LBS1 special education requires.

My research background instilled the analytical rigor that now drives my instructional practice — progress monitoring, data-driven IEP goal-setting, and systematic instruction design built on the same discipline as empirical inquiry. My psychology minor provided the behavioral and developmental foundation that informs how I read student behavior, design interventions, and apply FBA frameworks in real time. My time inside the Cook County criminal justice system — where documentation errors carry real consequences and institutional compliance and human dignity are in constant tension — prepared me for the legal and ethical weight of special education in ways that traditional teacher preparation programs cannot replicate.

Today, I am completing a Master of Arts in Teaching with an LBS1 endorsement through the Chicago Public Schools Teacher Residency Program at National Louis University. My current residency placement is in the cluster program at Lincoln Park High School, where I support students with intellectual and developmental disabilities ages 14 through 21 in an inclusive high school setting alongside transition-focused instruction grounded in systematic practice and community integration. I arrived at this work equipped — not just credentialed.

Architectural Pragmatism
"
My role as a special education teacher
is fundamentally architectural.

-- Catrina Wright

Architectural Pragmatism is the framework through which I design instruction. Architecture demands structural integrity — every material I build, every support I embed, and every goal I write must bear the weight of genuine student capacity. Pragmatism demands accountability to evidence — what data shows, what the student demonstrates, what the environment requires. Together, these commitments define a teaching practice built not on inspiration but on deliberate design. Every student who enters my classroom arrives with a vision for their own life. My job is not to supply that vision — it is to build the instructional conditions through which they can pursue it.

CPS Special Education Tool Ecosystem

I built the CPS Special Education Tool Ecosystem as the centerpiece of my SPE 587 Shark Tank presentation — a practicum-level design challenge requiring me to develop, deploy, and publicly defend a family-facing educational technology resource for Chicago Public Schools families. What began as a single IEP literacy tool grew into a cross-referenced, legally grounded system of four deployed applications. The justice-involved scaffold — built on verified federal citations and Illinois-specific statutory sources, informed by my prior work in the Cook County criminal justice system and direct engagement with organizations including Cabrini Green Legal Aid — is the thread that connects the four tools and makes them function as a system rather than as isolated resources. Every tool in the CPS Special Education Tool Ecosystem is bilingual, UDL-designed, and publicly deployed at no cost.

IEP Literacy · Bilingual · Role-Based
IEP Jargon Tool

A role-based, bilingual IEP literacy tool serving five user types — Parent/Guardian, Student, Teacher, Administrator, and Advocate. Built to replace the static paper packets that routinely exclude families from the IEP process. The justice-involved scaffold adds three new glossary terms and cross-referenced parent and advocate scripts for navigating discipline and detention. 24 bilingual terms. UDL-accessible.
